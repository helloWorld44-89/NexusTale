 # Novel Structure Templates
*A reference framework for AI-assisted novel writing*

This document describes common novel structures that authors can select, combine, or modify.
Each structure acts as a **story scaffold**, helping the AI guide plotting, pacing, and revisions
while allowing the author to remain in control.

---

## 1. Three-Act Structure
**Ideal for:** Most genres, broad audiences  
**Core principle:** Setup → Conflict → Resolution

### Acts
- **Act I – Setup**
  - Introduce protagonist, setting, and central problem
  - Inciting incident disrupts the status quo
- **Act II – Confrontation**
  - Escalating obstacles and complications
  - Midpoint revelation or reversal
- **Act III – Resolution**
  - Climax resolves the main conflict
  - Denouement establishes a new normal

**Strengths:** Flexible, intuitive, and widely understood  
**Risks:** Can feel predictable if beats are too rigid

---

## 2. Hero’s Journey (Monomyth)
**Ideal for:** Epic fantasy, sci‑fi, mythic or transformational stories  
**Core principle:** Personal transformation through departure, initiation, and return

### Common Beats
- Call to Adventure
- Refusal of the Call
- Mentor / Supernatural Aid
- Crossing the Threshold
- Trials, Allies, Enemies
- Ordeal (symbolic death)
- Revelation / Reward
- Return with the Elixir

**Strengths:** Powerful emotional and thematic arc  
**Risks:** Overuse and cliché if followed slavishly

---

## 3. Heist Structure
**Ideal for:** Crime, thrillers, capers, ensemble casts  
**Core principle:** Plan → Execute → Complicate → Reveal

### Core Phases
- Team assembly
- Explanation of the plan
- Execution and escalation
- Reversals and setbacks
- Twist revealing the *true* plan

**Strengths:** High tension, clever misdirection  
**Risks:** Requires airtight logic to avoid plot holes

---

## 4. Save the Cat Beat Sheet
**Ideal for:** Commercial fiction, fast-paced narratives  
**Core principle:** Emotional beats at precise story percentages

### Key Beats
- Opening Image
- Theme Stated
- Catalyst
- Debate
- Midpoint (false victory or defeat)
- All Is Lost
- Dark Night of the Soul
- Finale

**Strengths:** Excellent pacing and reader engagement  
**Risks:** Can feel mechanical if over-applied

---

## 5. Five-Act Structure
**Ideal for:** Literary fiction, tragedy, complex drama  
**Core principle:** Extended rise and fallout

### Acts
1. Exposition
2. Rising Action
3. Climax
4. Falling Action
5. Resolution

**Strengths:** Allows space for consequences and reflection  
**Risks:** Slower pacing; less suited to thrillers

---

## 6. Fichtean Curve
**Ideal for:** Thrillers, suspense, mysteries  
**Core principle:** Constant escalation via crises

### Structure
- Immediate inciting crisis
- Multiple escalating complications
- Minimal exposition
- Climactic resolution

**Strengths:** Relentless momentum  
**Risks:** Limited space for character introspection

---

## 7. Mystery / Investigation Structure
**Ideal for:** Detective fiction, noir, cozy mysteries  
**Core principle:** Question → Clues → Truth

### Typical Flow
- Crime or mystery introduced
- Investigation and false leads
- Rising danger
- Revelation of truth
- Justice, escape, or moral ambiguity

**Strengths:** Strong reader engagement through curiosity  
**Risks:** Ending must feel earned and logical

---

## 8. Spiral / Descent Structure
**Ideal for:** Horror, tragedy, psychological fiction  
**Core principle:** Gradual loss of control

### Arc
- Apparent stability
- Small moral or practical compromises
- Escalating consequences
- Irreversible collapse or transformation

**Strengths:** Creates dread and inevitability  
**Risks:** Emotionally heavy; bleak endings

---

## 9. Quest Structure
**Ideal for:** Adventure, fantasy  
**Core principle:** Reach a goal through episodic trials

### Elements
- Clear objective
- Series of obstacles or locations
- Final confrontation or revelation

**Strengths:** Easily expandable, intuitive  
**Risks:** Episodic weakness without strong character arc

---

## 10. Kishōtenketsu
**Ideal for:** Literary fiction, slice-of-life  
**Core principle:** Contrast rather than conflict

### Phases
1. Introduction
2. Development
3. Twist (contrast, not confrontation)
4. Harmony or synthesis

**Strengths:** Subtle, reflective, unconventional  
**Risks:** Low tension for conflict-driven readers

---

## 11. Multiple-POV / Braided Structure
**Ideal for:** Epics, political fiction, literary sagas  
**Core principle:** Separate arcs converge

### Structure
- Alternating perspectives
- Parallel thematic development
- Convergence or collision

**Strengths:** Depth and scope  
**Risks:** Reader confusion without discipline

---

## 12. Nonlinear / Mosaic Structure
**Ideal for:** Experimental or memory-driven narratives  
**Core principle:** Meaning emerges from arrangement

### Features
- Out-of-order scenes
- Emphasis on theme or emotion
- Gradual revelation

**Strengths:** Mirrors memory and trauma  
**Risks:** High cognitive load for readers

---

# Freestyle Mode
Authors may define:
- Custom acts or phases
- Named beats
- Structural rules
- Borrowed elements from multiple frameworks

The AI should treat this as a **living outline**, not a constraint.
